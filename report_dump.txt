Attention Is Not All You Need: Beating Hallucinations Using Causal
Dynamic Routing
Yaawar Askari, Jay Agarwal, Abhimanyu Bhowmik, Aman Agarwal, Dhruv Kumar,
BITS Pilani
Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) often generate
hallucinations, especially in abstractive summa-
rization and open-set question answering. This
undermines their reliability in real-world appli-
cations where factual consistency is critical.
Existing approaches rely largely on output-
based evaluation or post-hoc verification,
which provide limited insight into the inter-
nal mechanisms that give rise to hallucinations.
Furthermore, most previous work identifies cor-
relational signals without isolating which inter-
nal components causally contribute to halluci-
nated outputs.
In this paper, we propose a dynamic inference-
time routing framework that uses model-
internal signals to detect and mitigate halluci-
nations in LLMs during generation. We use se-
mantic entropy probes to estimate uncertainty,
and attention-based grounding signals to guide
selective suppression of attention heads that are
not grounded with the context. We evaluate our
approach on summarization and question an-
swering benchmarks using standard factuality
and QA metrics such as XSum, Natural Ques-
tions, TriviaQA, and SQuAD, using Llama-2-
7B. Our results show that internal signals can
enable effective single-pass hallucination detec-
tion and mitigation without external retrieval or
multiple decoding passes.
1 Introduction
Large Language Models (LLMs) have rapidly ad-
vanced the state of the art in natural language pro-
cessing, showing strong performance on summa-
rization, question answering, and reasoning tasks.
Despite this, LLMs frequently hallucinate, gen-
erating outputs that are factually incorrect or un-
supported by the input context (Yu et al., 2024;
Chuang et al., 2024). This is a critical problem
in applications such as summarization and open-
domain question answering, where factual accu-
racy is essential for reliability and user trust (Wang
et al., 2025). The frequency of hallucinations in
LLMs limits their use in high-stakes domains such
as journalism, healthcare, and knowledge retrieval
(Kossen et al., 2024).
In this work, we investigate the detection and
mitigation of hallucinations in both open-domain
question answering and abstractive summarization.
The goal is to produce outputs that are factually
grounded in the input context or external knowl-
edge given an input document or question. How-
ever, LLMs frequently rely on spurious correlations
or parametric knowledge, which results in content
that is either fabricated or unsupported (Yu et al.,
2024; Chuang et al., 2024). It can be difficult to
detect hallucinations because outputs can seem re-
alistic, and standard overlap-based metrics such
as ROUGE do not adequately capture factual con-
sistency (Yu et al., 2024). Post-hoc detection is
insufficient because hallucinations can arise during
intermediate stages of generation, before they are
observable in the final output. Thus, it is necessary
to intervene at inference-time and take action to
mitigate hallucinations before inaccurate content
is generated for users (Wang et al., 2025; Kossen
et al., 2024).
Existing approaches to hallucination detection
in LLMs largely rely on reference-based metrics
or external verification models and operate at the
output level. While these methods can identify
factual inconsistencies, they do not provide in-
sight into the internal mechanisms of LLMs and
are not suitable for real-time mitigation. Recent
work has explored internal signals such as uncer-
tainty estimation (Kossen et al., 2024) and patterns
from attention maps (Chuang et al., 2024). Se-
mantic entropy methods approximate uncertainty
by measuring variability across multiple genera-
tions (Kossen et al., 2024), while attention-based
approaches quantify the model’s reliance on input
context (Chuang et al., 2024). Mechanistic inter-
pretability studies further analyze the role of spe-

In this paper, we propose a dynamic inference
routing framework that uses model-internal sig-
nals to identify and reduce hallucinations as they
are being generated. We estimate model uncer-
tainty with a lightweight semantic entropy probe
trained on hidden states (Kossen et al., 2024), and
combine it with attention-based grounding signals
that quantify dependence on input context (Chuang
et al., 2024). When the model is expected to be
in a high uncertainty state, we dynamically ad-
just the model’s behaviour by suppressing atten-
tion heads with low context reliance, while context-
grounded components retain their influence (Tong
et al., 2025). This enables targeted hallucination
mitigation at inference time, without extra decod-
ing passes or external retrieval. We incorporate
causal validation techniques, including ablation
and blindness tests, to identify components that di-
rectly contribute to hallucinated outputs (Yu et al.,
2024).
We evaluate our approach across both summa-
rization and question answering benchmarks, in-
cluding XSum, Natural Questions, TriviaQA, and
SQuAD. Following prior work on semantic entropy
probes (Kossen et al., 2024), we train probes across
all hidden layers and identify the layers that provide
the strongest predictive signals for hallucination.
Contributions.Our main contributions are as
follows:
• Unified Internal-Signal Framework:We
combine Semantic Entropy Probes (SEP) for

(Chuang et al., 2024) into a unified, single-

out retraining the base model. Prior work
studies these signals separately: SEPs ex-

(Kossen et al., 2024), while Lookback Lens
models contextual grounding using attention
ratios (Chuang et al., 2024). We show that
uncertainty-based signals (SEP) and attention-

2 Related Work
2.1 Hallucination Detection and Uncertainty
Estimation
Till now, hallucination detection in LLMs has
largely relied on output-based evaluation and un-
certainty estimation. Metrics such as ROUGE fail
to capture factual consistency in abstractive tasks.
Recent approaches estimate uncertainty via seman-
tic entropy, which quantifies output variability in
semantic space. To reduce computational cost, Se-
mantic Entropy Probes (SEP) predict semantic en-
tropy directly from hidden states, demonstrating
that hallucination signals are internally encoded
within the model (Kossen et al., 2024). However,
these methods remain largely correlational and of-
ten require post-hoc analysis, limiting their appli-
cability in real-time settings.
We leverage single-pass internal signals to en-
able both detection and intervention during infer-
ence, moving beyond post-hoc or multi-sample ap-
proaches.
2.2 Attention-Based Grounding and
Interpretability
Attention-based methods analyze how LLMs uti-
lize context during generation. The lookback ratio
measures attention to source tokens versus gener-
ated tokens, showing that reduced context reliance
correlates with hallucination (Chuang et al., 2024).
Extensions include attention editing methods that
modify attention maps during inference (Wang
et al., 2025) and head-level gating approaches that
dynamically reweight attention contributions (Tong
et al., 2025) to improve grounding.
Mechanistic interpretability studies further identify
internal components responsible for model behav-
ior. Techniques such as logit lens and causal me-
diation analysis link specific attention heads and
MLP layers to output predictions (Yu et al., 2024).
While these approaches provide insight into the
internal causes of hallucination, they are largely
diagnostic and not designed for adaptive control
during generation.
We integrate grounding signals (Chuang et al.,
2024) with uncertainty estimates derived from Se-
mantic Entropy Probes (Kossen et al., 2024) to
enable dynamic, input-dependent intervention at
inference time.
2.3 Evaluation Benchmarks for Faithfulness
and QA
Hallucination evaluation has been studied across
multiple benchmarks in both summarization and
question answering. In summarization, highly ab-
stractive datasets are commonly used because they
encourage models to generate novel content and
increase the risk of hallucinations (Chuang et al.,
2024; Wang et al., 2025). Faithfulness evaluation in
this setting remains challenging, as overlap-based
metrics such as ROUGE do not fully capture fac-
tual accuracy (Yu et al., 2024).
In question answering, standard extractive and
open-domain benchmarks evaluate correctness us-
ing exact match and F1 scores. However, these
metrics do not explicitly capture hallucination, es-
pecially when models generate plausible but un-
supported answers (Kossen et al., 2024; Yu et al.,
2024). More recent work has proposed uncertainty-
based detection (Kossen et al., 2024) and attention-
based evaluation methods (Chuang et al., 2024),
as well as attention intervention strategies (Wang
et al., 2025).
Despite these advances, existing benchmarks are
primarily designed for evaluation rather than miti-
gation, and they do not incorporate internal model
signals or causal analysis (Yu et al., 2024). More-
over, most hallucination studies focus on a single
task, limiting understanding of how hallucinations
generalize across domains.
We perform a unified evaluation across summa-
rization and question answering benchmarks and
focus on inference-time mitigation using internal
uncertainty (Kossen et al., 2024) and grounding
signals (Chuang et al., 2024), bridging the gap be-
tween evaluation and intervention.
3 Methodology
3.1 Overview
We combine hallucination detection with targeted
attention intervention. First, we label data using
semantic entropy computed from clustered stochas-
tic generations (Kossen et al., 2024), and train a
lightweight SEP on hidden states to predict hallu-
cination risk. Next, we extract LR features from
attention to measure how strongly each head relies
on the input context (Chuang et al., 2024). Finally,
we apply a head-adaptive gating mechanism that
suppresses low-grounded attention heads during

the SEP indicates high hallucination risk.
3.2 Semantic Entropy Probe
In order to prepare the dataset for training our Se-
mantic Entropy Probe (SEP), we sample 2000 ex-
amples from datasets such as XSum or SQuAD.
Next, we generate 10 stochastic summaries per ex-
ample using the Meta Llama2-7B model. Then,
we use DeBERTa as an NLI model to cluster the
generated answers by semantic equivalence follow-
ing prior semantic entropy estimation procedures
(Kossen et al., 2024). Samples showing bidirec-
tional entailment are assigned to the same cluster.
Further, we calculate the semantic entropy over
these clusters. Samples with high agreement (>
0.7) are labeled Confident (0), while those with
low agreement (< 0.3) are labeled Hallucinated (1).
Ambiguous samples are discarded.
Then, we perform a prompt-only forward pass on
each filtered sample and extract the hidden state
of the last token from the final transformer layer.
We then train an L2-regularized logistic regression
classifier on the normalized hidden state features,
in order to predict hallucinations. We then evaluate
the obtained classifier on the rest of the dataset, and
obtain the best series of layers ( >= 5) which give
the best AUROC score in detecting hallucinations.
3.3 Lookback Ratio Feature Extraction
We extract lookback ratio (LR) features to quantify
how strongly each attention head grounds its pre-
dictions in the input context (Chuang et al., 2024).
For each sample, we run the model with attention
outputs enabled, collecting attention weights at ev-
ery generation step. At each layerland attention
headh, we compute the proportion of attention
mass assigned to prompt tokens relative to the total
attention mass over all tokens (prompt + generated),
yielding a per-step grounding score. These scores
are averaged across all generated tokens to obtain
a stable estimate LRl,h ∈[0,1] , where higher val-
ues indicate stronger reliance on the input context.
The resulting per-head scores are flattened into a
fixed-length feature vector of sizeL x Hfor each
sample. These features are paired with semantic
entropy labels and used to drive inference-time in-
terventions that selectively suppress low-lookback
(i.e., less grounded) attention heads.
3.4 Head-Adaptive Gating Using Lookback
Ratio
We introduce a head-adaptive gating mechanism
that selectively modulates attention heads during
generation based on their grounding behavior, in-
spired by prior head-level modulation strategies
(Tong et al., 2025). Specifically, at each decod-
ing step, we compute the lookback ratio LRl,h for
every attention head. This produces a per-head
grounding score in [0,1], where higher values in-
dicate stronger reliance on the input context. We
then transform this score into a multiplicative gate
applied directly to the head’s contribution to the
attention output. We introduce two variants of this
multiplicative gate - a soft variant, and a hard vari-
ant. In the soft variant, the gate is defined as:

attention flow.
4 Experimental Setup
4.1 Datasets
We evaluate our approach on four widely used NLP
benchmarks spanning summarization and question
answering. XSum is an abstractive summariza-
tion dataset consisting of BBC news articles paired
with single-sentence summaries, designed to test
extreme compression and factual consistency. Nat-
ural Questions (NQ) is a question answering dataset
derived from real user queries issued to a search
engine, with answers grounded in Wikipedia pas-
sages, emphasizing open-domain QA. TriviaQA
contains trivia-style questions with evidence docu-
ments from the web, requiring multi sentence rea-
soning and answer extraction. SQuAD is a read-
ing comprehension dataset based on Wikipedia ar-
ticles, where models must extract answer spans

4.2 Models
We conduct our experiments using two open-
weight large language models from the LLaMA
family. Specifically, we use LLaMA 2-7B and
LLaMA 3-8B-Instruct for both the abstract summa-
rization and question-answering tasks. The models
are used in a few shot inference setting, where they
are provided 5 question-answer (or 3 summariza-
tion) examples along with the actual question/data
to summarize.
4.3 Evaluation Metrics
We evaluate our method using a combination of
generation quality and detection metrics. ROUGE
is used to approximate semantic consistency in the
context of semantic entropy. To select the most
informative hidden layer for the Semantic Entropy
Probe (SEP), we use AUROC, which measures
the model’s ability to discriminate between halluci-
nated and factual outputs. For downstream evalua-
tion, we report token-level F1 score to capture the
balance between precision and recall at the token
level, along with accuracy to measure exact token
prediction correctness.
4.4 Implementation Details
We use a few-shot prompting strategy with all
evaluated models, with 5 examples given for each
QA task generation, and 3 examples given for ev-
ery summarization task generation. For sampling
question-answer pairs from the selected datasets for
training the SEP, we use a random seed of 20. We
set max_new_tokens = 50 for generating responses
for evaluation on QA tasks, and max_new_tokens
= 100 on summarization tasks. When models are
used in a high temperature setting, the tempera-
ture is set to 1.0. Otherwise, if they are used in a
low-temperature setting, it is set to 0.1.
5 Results and Discussion
5.1 Cross-Dataset Performance
Objective.This experiment evaluates whether
the proposed Semantic Entropy Probe (SEP) learns
generalizable hallucination signals that transfer
across datasets. Specifically, we assess whether
SEP trained on one dataset maintains strong perfor-
mance when applied to out-of-distribution datasets,
and quantify the extent of performance degradation
under cross-domain transfer.
Findings.We evaluate the generalization of the
Semantic Entropy Probe (SEP) using a cross-
dataset AUROC matrix. When trained and eval-
uated on the same dataset (in-distribution), SEP
achieves a mean AUROC of 0.7752. Under cross-
dataset transfer (out-of-distribution), performance
degrades moderately to an average AUROC of
0.7227. While some transfer pairs (e.g., SQuAD to
TriviaQA) retain strong performance, others exhibit
larger drops, indicating partial domain sensitivity.
Overall, these results demonstrate that SEP cap-
tures generalizable hallucination signals and is able
to detect hallucinations outside its in-domain data,
while still benefiting from in-domain calibration.
5.2 Ablation Study
Objective.The ablation study evaluates the
causal role of grounding-aware attention heads and
the effectiveness of head-adaptive gating. Specif-
ically, it investigates (1) whether selectively sup-
pressing low lookback-ratio heads improves robust-
ness compared to indiscriminate removal, and (2)
whether high lookback-ratio heads are causally re-
sponsible for accurate, grounded predictions.
Findings.Results show that soft, sigmoid-based
gating consistently outperforms or matches hard
knockout of all heads, indicating that selective sup-
pression preserves useful information while miti-
gating ungrounded contributions. Knockout experi-
ments demonstrate that indiscriminate removal of
attention heads leads to larger performance degra-
dation, supporting the need for fine-grained control.
Blindness tests further reveal that removing high
lookback-ratio heads significantly reduces accuracy
on multiple datasets, confirming their causal role in
grounding model predictions. Overall, the findings
validate the effectiveness of head-adaptive gating
and highlight the importance of grounding-aware
attention in hallucination mitigation.

5.3 Discussion
Our results demonstrate that hallucination in large
language models can be effectively detected and
mitigated using internal signals without requiring
external verification. SEPs provide a lightweight
and generalizable mechanism for identifying hal-
lucination risk from hidden states, while LR fea-
tures reveal interpretable grounding patterns at the
attention-head level. Together, these components
enable targeted intervention via head-adaptive gat-
ing, which selectively suppresses ungrounded at-
tention heads. Across datasets, we observe that this
combination maintains strong performance while
improving robustness, highlighting the importance
of internal model dynamics in controlling halluci-
nations.
These findings suggest a shift from post-hoc ver-
ification toward inference-time control of model
behavior. By leveraging signals already present
within the model, our approach avoids the over-
head of additional models or external knowledge
sources, making it efficient and broadly applica-
ble. The use of attention-based grounding signals
further opens avenues for interpretable and con-
trollable generation, where specific components of
the model can be modulated based on predicted
risk. This points to a promising direction for build-
ing more reliable language models through internal
monitoring and adaptive computation.
6 Conclusion
We present a lightweight, inference-time frame-
work for hallucination detection and mitigation
that combines a Semantic Entropy Probe (SEP)
with grounding-aware attention control. SEP pre-
dicts hallucination risk from hidden states, while
lookback ratio (LR) features quantify how strongly
attention heads rely on the input context. We use
these signals to apply head-adaptive gating, selec-
tively suppressing ungrounded attention heads dur-
ing generation. Across four datasets, our method
demonstrates strong generalization (OOD/ID AU-
ROC ratio of 0.93) and causal effectiveness, show-
ing that grounding-aware attention modulation can
improve reliability without degrading performance.
Our results highlight the value of internal model
signals for efficient and interpretable control of
generation, offering an alternative to post-hoc or
external verification approaches.
Future work will extend this approach to larger
models, additional tasks, and more robust gating
strategies, as well as explore combining internal
signals with external knowledge for improved fac-
tuality.
Limitations
• Dataset limitations:Our experiments
are limited to English-language datasets
(XSum, SQuAD, TriviaQA, and Natural Ques-
tions), which primarily consist of news and
Wikipedia-based content. These datasets do
not capture low-resource languages, diverse
dialects, or domain-specific knowledge, and
may exhibit annotation noise or bias. Addi-
tionally, the sampled subset used for seman-
tic entropy labeling is relatively small, which
may limit coverage of rare or complex hallu-
cination cases.
